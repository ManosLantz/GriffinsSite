The History of Rugby

From a schoolyard in 19th-century England to stadiums across the world — and the pitch at Lido Soccer.

Rugby is a sport built on courage, teamwork and respect. Its roots stretch back nearly two centuries to the playing fields of England, and the game has since grown into one of the world's great team sports — played on every continent and loved fiercely wherever it lands.

A Legendary Beginning

The most famous origin story dates to 1823, when — according to legend — a pupil at Rugby School named William Webb Ellis, "with a fine disregard for the rules of football as played in his time, first took the ball in his arms and ran with it." Whether the tale is fact or romantic myth, the school gave the game both its name and its earliest identity.

"…with a fine disregard for the rules of football as played in his time, first took the ball in his arms and ran with it."

Writing the Laws

For decades, every school and club played its own version of "football," with wildly different rules. In 1845, pupils at Rugby School wrote down the first formal laws of their handling game. The sport became properly organised in 1871, when the Rugby Football Union (RFU) was founded in London and the first international match — Scotland against England — was played in Edinburgh.

A Global Game

Carried by schools, universities, soldiers and sailors, rugby spread rapidly across the British Empire and beyond. It took deep root in New Zealand, South Africa, Australia, Wales, Ireland and France, each nation adding its own flavour and fierce rivalries that endure to this day.

The Great Split

In 1895, a dispute over paying players for time lost at work — known as "broken time" — caused a historic schism. A group of northern English clubs broke away to form the Northern Union, which evolved into the distinct code of Rugby League. The original game, governed by the RFU, became known as Rugby Union and remained strictly amateur until it finally turned professional in 1995.

Rugby Today

The first Rugby World Cup was held in 1987, and its trophy — the Webb Ellis Cup — honours that founding legend. Rugby Sevens, the fast-paced seven-a-side format, joined the Olympic Games in 2016, introducing the sport to millions of new fans. And here in Heraklion, the Griffins proudly carry that long tradition forward, one ruck, maul and try at a time.
